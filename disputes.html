What this work involves
Pragmatic, not combative.

Disputes tend to arrive at the worst possible moment.

When you're busy. When the asset needs focus elsewhere. When the last thing you want is a fight. They also tend to escalate if left. A disagreement that was a manageable conversation at the start of the year can become a formal, expensive matter by the end of it, once positions have hardened.

My approach is pragmatic, not combative.

A technically correct position isn't always the commercially sensible one, and forcing a dispute to formal proceedings is often the most expensive way to achieve a worse outcome. Good dispute work is about reading the situation clearly, understanding where leverage genuinely sits, and finding the settlement that protects your position without destroying the relationship or the value of the asset.

Who this is for
Landlords with a problem.

Landlords dealing with a difficult tenant. A disagreement that's starting to harden. A situation where you're not sure how to handle the next conversation.

If something has gone wrong and you need a plain read on where you stand before it escalates, get in touch.

How I approach it.

Six principles that shape every dispute instruction I take on, from the first conversation through to settlement.

01
Assess the position early.
Strength of your argument, strength of theirs, evidence, likely outcomes. A plain read before strategy.
02
Advise on leverage honestly.
Where you've got a strong hand, I'll say so. Where you haven't, I'll say that too, and suggest an alternative route.
03
Negotiate, not posture.
Direct discussions with the tenant or their advisor, aimed at resolution. Letters fly less than people talking.
04
Coordinate with legal advisors.
Where the dispute needs formal input, working alongside your solicitor or one from my network. Costs controlled, strategy aligned.
05
Settle cleanly when the terms are right.
A good settlement resolves the issue, closes the exposure, and lets both parties move on. Not a partial fix, not a half-truth.
06
Keep the asset in mind throughout.
A dispute that damages the tenancy relationship or triggers a void can cost more than the original issue. Resolution that preserves value, and keeps the asset performing, is always the objective.
An honest position

Fight only what's worth fighting.

Some disputes are worth fighting. Some aren't. The most valuable thing I can do early is tell you which, before strategy hardens around a position that costs more than it returns.
