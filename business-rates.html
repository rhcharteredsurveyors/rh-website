What this work involves
Real challenges, real evidence.

Business rates are one of the biggest ongoing costs of owning commercial property.

And they're frequently wrong. Rateable values are set by the Valuation Office Agency using methods and measurements that regularly contain errors. The appeal process (Check, Challenge, Appeal) is specifically designed for landlords and occupiers to push back where the assessment doesn't stack up.

My role is to tell you honestly whether your assessment is worth challenging, and to run the process properly when it is.

Equally importantly, I'll tell you when it isn't. An appeal that fails costs you time, fees, and sometimes a higher assessment if the VOA revisits the valuation. Knowing when not to challenge is often more valuable than knowing when to.

Who this is for
Landlords and owner-occupiers.

Landlords with vacant or transitional assets where rates exposure is a real cost. Property owners who've had a revaluation and aren't sure whether to challenge it. Owner-occupiers facing a liability that feels wrong but haven't had an independent view on whether it actually is.

If you want a plain read on whether your assessment is worth challenging, get in touch.

How I approach it.

Six principles that shape every rates instruction I take on, from initial review through to final settlement.

01
Review before you commit.
A proper assessment of whether the rateable value is likely to be excessive, based on measurement, classification, methodology, and comparable evidence.
02
An honest cost-benefit review.
Likely saving versus likely time, fees, and risk. No appeal pursued for appeal's sake, and no fee charged for work that won't deliver value.
03
Run the CCA process properly.
Check stage, Challenge stage, Appeal if needed. Evidence built correctly at each step, in the format the VOA actually requires.
04
Manage the billing position too.
Sometimes the work isn't only about the assessment. It's about engaging with the local authority on backdated liability, billing start dates, and ensuring the rating record matches the property.
05
Mitigation where it matters.
For void periods, transitional relief, empty property strategy, or repositioning during refurbishment. The work covers more than just appeals.
06
Know when not to act.
Some assessments are broadly right. Some are marginally high but not high enough to justify challenge. And some properties are underassessed, where a challenge could trigger an increase. I'll tell you which.
An honest position

Knowing when not to challenge.

Some properties are actually underassessed. A tenant who's carried out a high-quality fit-out, or an owner whose property has been improved beyond what the rating list reflects, can sit at a rateable value lower than the premises would warrant. In those cases, the last thing you want is to invite a reassessment.

I'll tell you honestly when that risk is live, so you don't accidentally trigger an increase by trying to secure a reduction.
