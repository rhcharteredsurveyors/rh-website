What this work involves
Pragmatic, not combative.

Disputes arrive at the worst possible moment.

When your business needs your focus elsewhere. When the last thing you want is a fight. When you're not sure what leverage you've actually got. They also tend to escalate if left. A disagreement that was a manageable conversation at the start of the year can become a formal, expensive matter by the end of it, once positions have hardened.

My approach is pragmatic, not combative.

A technically correct position isn't always the commercially sensible one. Sometimes you've got strong grounds and the work is pressing them. Sometimes you haven't, and the real job is pivoting to a different settlement that protects what matters. Good dispute work is about reading the situation honestly, understanding where leverage genuinely sits, and finding the outcome that protects your position without destroying your business.

Who this is for
Tenants facing a landlord dispute.

Tenants dealing with a landlord dispute, whether it's just starting or has already hardened into something more formal. Businesses facing dilapidations claims, service charge challenges, repair disagreements, or a break clause dispute.

Anyone who's not sure what leverage they actually have and wants a plain read before they respond. If something has gone wrong and you need independent advice, get in touch.

How I approach it.

Six principles that shape every tenant dispute instruction I take on, from the first conversation through to settlement.

01
Assess the position properly.
Strength of your argument, strength of the landlord's, evidence, likely outcomes. A plain read before any strategy.
02
Be honest about leverage.
Where you've got a strong hand, I'll say so. Where you haven't, I'll say that too, and suggest a different route.
03
Negotiate, don't posture.
Direct discussions aimed at resolution rather than winning. The point is the outcome, not the satisfaction of being right.
04
Coordinate with legal advisors.
Where formal input is needed, working with your solicitor or one from my network. Costs controlled, strategy aligned.
05
Know when to pivot.
Sometimes the original position isn't winnable but a different outcome is. The best settlements often aren't the one either side walked in expecting.
06
Settle cleanly when the terms are right.
A good settlement resolves the issue, closes the exposure, and lets you get back to running your business. Not a partial fix, not a half-truth.
An honest position

Sometimes winning means pivoting.

The original position you walked in with isn't always the one that delivers value. Strong facts can sit alongside a weak legal route. The best dispute outcomes usually come from advisors prepared to recognise that early, and re-shape the negotiation toward what's actually achievable.
