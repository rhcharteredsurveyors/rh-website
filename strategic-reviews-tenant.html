A Strategic Review is often the easiest way to start working together. You get a considered view on a specific situation. I get to understand how you think.

If further work flows from it, it flows.

What this work involves
An honest read of where you stand.

Sometimes the right next step isn't obvious.

The rent review proposal on the table is troubling but you're not sure whether it's actually high. You've been offered a new lease and can't tell if the terms are fair. You've been approached about an early exit and don't know whether to engage or resist. You're weighing up relocation versus renewal and need someone who'll look at the whole picture, not just the deal in front of you.

In those moments, what you need isn't a transaction.

You need focused, independent advice that looks at where you actually stand, tells you plainly what the options are, and helps you decide what to do. That's what a Strategic Review is.

Who this is for
Tenants with a decision to make.

Tenants facing a decision that isn't straightforward. A proposal that looks wrong, but you're not sure if it is. An offer you don't know whether to accept. A situation where you'd value a plain, independent read before you commit to a position.

If you're wrestling with something and want an outside view, get in touch.

How I approach it.

Six principles that shape every Strategic Review I undertake for tenants, regardless of sector or property type.

01
Understand what you're actually deciding.
The framing of the question shapes the usefulness of the answer. The first conversation is usually about getting that framing right.
02
Look at the full picture.
Lease terms, market evidence, business context, alternative options, realistic outcomes. Not just the immediate question, but everything around it.
03
Name what matters.
What's actually driving the decision, where the risks sit, what the real options are. The signal, separated from the noise.
04
Show the working.
You don't just get the conclusion. You get the reasoning, the evidence, and the assumptions underneath it. So you can challenge it, build on it, or revisit it later.
05
Give you a plain recommendation.
A review that ends in "it depends" isn't a review. I'll tell you what I think, why, and what I'd do if it were my business.
06
Sometimes the advice is "don't".
Some positions look fightable but aren't. Some offers look worse than they are. The value of independent advice is hearing that plainly, before you spend money pursuing a weak case.
