When you hire me, you get me.

Most commercial property decisions cross disciplines. An adviser who can see the full picture, and who stays on the file from first conversation to final outcome, produces better outcomes than one who can only see a slice.

1st April 2026 6 min read
Section 01
Why the practice is structured this way

RH Chartered Surveyors was founded on a deliberate principle. One experienced surveyor handles each instruction from start to finish. There is no team beneath me handling the day-to-day. There is no partner I introduce and then withdraw from. When you instruct me, you get me.

That is a choice, not a constraint. I worked in larger firms before founding the practice, and I understand what those structures offer. They have real strengths. They also have a structural problem that affects the quality of advice in ways the firms themselves do not always recognise. The practice exists in its current form because I think that problem matters.

This article sets out what that problem is and why a founder-led structure addresses it.

Section 02
The thing property does that catches firms out

Property decisions do not stay in their lane.

Almost every meaningful commercial property decision touches more than one discipline. A lease renewal is not just an opportunity to review the rent. It is also a valuation question, an asset management question, a portfolio strategy question, and frequently a disposal planning question. The right answer on rent depends on what the landlord intends to do with the property in five years. The right answer on lease length depends on the tenant covenant and the wider letting market. The right answer on break clauses depends on the asset strategy.

That cross-disciplinary view affects how an instruction is approached from day one, not just how the relationship develops over time. A client who brings me a rent review gets advice on the rent review. The broader experience also means that if I notice something in the course of that work, whether the rates position looks worth a closer look, whether a lease term is creating future risk, whether the positioning of the asset sits awkwardly against a likely disposal in a few years, I will raise it. Not as scope creep, and not as work I am committing to do unbidden, but as a flag for the client to consider whether it warrants attention.

In a firm organised around specialist teams, those connections are harder to make on a single instruction. The rent review surveyor handles the rent review. The other questions sit with other teams, and the specialist instructed to handle one piece of work is not usually going to pull in another team to weigh in on something the client did not ask about. Each conversation is competent on its own terms. The connections between them are where things tend to slip, and the slip happens on the very first instruction, not just over years of separate ones.

A factor that should have been weighed in the rent review gets noticed only when the asset is sold. A lease term that should have been engineered differently becomes obvious at the next renewal. A rates assessment that should have been challenged or accepted on different grounds is left as it is. The cumulative effect of those small gaps is meaningful, both within a single instruction and across the life of a long-held asset.

Section 03
What changes when one person holds the file

The benefit is immediate, and it compounds.

When the same adviser handles a single instruction with a cross-disciplinary view, the benefit shows up at the point of advice. Issues that sit adjacent to the instructed work get flagged when they would otherwise have been missed. A rent review is handled with the rates position in mind. A lease renewal is handled with disposal planning in mind. A valuation considers what the asset management strategy is doing to the income profile.

That benefit then compounds when the same adviser handles successive instructions. The context carries forward. The decisions made at lease grant inform the approach at first review. The history of the review informs the renewal. The renewal feeds into disposal planning. None of that has to be reconstructed each time, because the same person already holds it.

A rent review handled in isolation might land at a defensible figure that is still suboptimal in the context of a planned sale eighteen months later. The surveyor who knows about the planned sale handles the review differently. The surveyor who is just handed a rent review without that context cannot, even if they would do the rent review itself competently.

The value is not just that you avoid repeating yourself. It is that decisions are made in the context of everything else that is going on with the asset.

This is the structural answer to the structural problem. Specialist teams are good at their specialism. They are less good at acting on information that sits outside it, particularly when they have not been asked to. One adviser who carries the whole picture closes that gap from the first instruction onwards.

Section 04
The trade-offs, honestly

What this structure does not do.

A founder-led practice is not the right structure for every instruction. There are situations where a larger firm is genuinely the better choice, and it is worth being clear about them.

  • Scale. A national portfolio with hundreds of assets needs the bandwidth of a larger firm. One person cannot manage that volume, and pretending otherwise serves no one.
  • Specialist niches outside my competence. Building surveying-led dilapidations, expert witness work on major arbitrations, specialist valuation in regulated sectors, all of these sit with people who do this work all day every day. I do not, and I will say so.
  • Conflict of interest. I act for either the landlord or the tenant on any given matter, never both. Where my existing engagements would create a conflict, the right thing is to say so and recommend someone else.

The right adviser for a piece of work is not always me. The practice positioning is that I will tell you that honestly, rather than taking on work that would be better done elsewhere. Right person, right job.

Section 05
What this looks like in practice

For the clients the practice is built around, the experience is straightforward. The first conversation is with me. The advice on the matter is from me. The negotiation is run by me. The final outcome is the one I have been working towards from the start.

What that means in practice is that even a single, discrete instruction is approached with a wider view of the property and the position. I will deal with what I have been asked to deal with. Where something else is worth raising, I will mention it, leaving the decision on whether to act on it with the client. Some flags will be things to address now. Some will be things to monitor. Some will be things to plan for, or to take separate advice on. The intent is to give the client the wider read, not to assume the wider work.

When the same client comes back later with a different instruction, the relationship and the context carry across. The new work starts from where the last work ended, not from scratch. The benefit that was immediate on the first instruction compounds from there.

That is what a founder-led practice means in the day-to-day. It is not a marketing position. It is the structural reason the practice was set up the way it was.
