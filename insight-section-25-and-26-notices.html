Home Insights Section 25 and Section 26 notices
Lease Renewal

Whoever serves first shapes the deal.

The choice of when and how to serve a renewal notice is often the most consequential decision in the whole process. Sitting on the question is itself a decision, and it is usually the one that gives away control.

29th April 2026 7 min read
Section 01
The procedural framework

Where a commercial lease has security of tenure under the Landlord and Tenant Act 1954, the lease does not simply expire at the end of its contractual term. It continues until either party serves a statutory notice to end or renew it. Two notices do that work. Section 25 is served by the landlord. Section 26 is served by the tenant.

The notices are usually described in terms of their procedural function: setting a date, opening the formal renewal process, triggering the statutory timetable. All of that is correct. But it underplays something more important.

The choice of whether to serve, when to serve, and which form of notice to use, materially affects the position of both parties for the remainder of the renewal. It is a strategic decision dressed up as a procedural one. The party that recognises that, and acts on it, usually ends up in the stronger position.

Section 02
What each notice does

The mechanics, briefly.

A Section 25 notice is the landlord's mechanism for ending or renewing the lease. It must specify a termination date, which has to be not less than six months and not more than twelve months from the date the notice is served, and not earlier than the contractual expiry date of the lease. The notice must also state whether the landlord opposes a new lease, and if so, on which of the statutory grounds.

A Section 26 notice is the tenant's equivalent mechanism. It requests a new lease from a specified date, again between six and twelve months from service, and again not earlier than the contractual expiry. A Section 26 notice sets out the tenant's proposed terms for the new lease, including the rent the tenant proposes to pay.

The party that serves first effectively closes the door on the other party's ability to serve. The two notices cannot operate at the same time. Once one is in force, the other cannot be served on the same lease.

Section 03
Why the timing matters

The notice sets the clock.

A statutory notice does more than open negotiations. It anchors the timeline. The termination or new lease date in the notice fixes the point at which the lease will end or the new lease will begin. That date in turn fixes the valuation date for the new rent, which is then established on the same market evidence principles that apply to any rent review.

The valuation date matters because market conditions shift. A property valued in a strengthening market produces one figure; the same property valued six months later in the same market produces a different figure. Where rental values are rising, an earlier valuation date generally favours the tenant. Where values are falling or static, an earlier date generally favours the landlord. The party choosing the date is choosing the market conditions under which the rent will be set.

Serving first is not just about being procedurally tidy. It is about choosing the conditions under which the next several years of rent will be agreed.

The same logic applies to the broader strategic position. A landlord planning a redevelopment or repositioning of the property has a reason to serve early on grounds of opposition. A tenant who has invested heavily in fit-out and wants certainty has a reason to serve a Section 26 with proposed terms favourable to them. Where both parties are content to drift, the lease continues on the existing terms by default, and the rent the tenant pays is the rent under the existing lease, which may now be materially below the market.

There is also the question of interim rent. Once renewal proceedings have been triggered by either a Section 25 or a Section 26, either party can apply to the court to determine an interim rent that applies for the period between the contractual expiry of the existing lease and the start of the new one. This matters because the interim rent does not have to wait for the new lease to be finalised. It can take effect from the earliest date the notice could have specified, which is usually close to the contractual expiry, and the calculation depends on which type of notice was served and which party applied for the interim rent. Where market rents have moved against the existing rent, the difference between the contractual rent and the interim rent can be significant, and the party slower to act is usually the one paying or receiving less than the market would support.

Section 04
Leases outside the Act

If the lease has been contracted out.

Not every commercial lease benefits from security of tenure under the 1954 Act. Where the lease has been validly contracted out at the start, the tenant has no statutory right to a new lease. The tenancy ends on the contractual expiry date, and the tenant must vacate unless new terms have been agreed.

The strategic position for a tenant on a contracted-out lease is therefore the inverse of one with security of tenure. There is no statutory framework to fall back on, no automatic continuation, and no Section 26 mechanism to request renewal. The tenant either negotiates a new lease with the landlord on terms they agree to, or they leave.

The further this conversation is left, the weaker the tenant position becomes. A tenant approaching the last six months of a contracted-out lease with no agreed renewal in place is in a materially weaker negotiating position than the same tenant eighteen months out. A tenant on a contracted-out lease who wants to stay should be raising the conversation early, not late. The landlord may agree terms readily, may push hard on rent, or may have already decided to take the property back. The tenant is better off knowing which of those is the case while there is still time to act on it.

Section 05
Reading the strategic position

The questions to ask before serving.

A decision to serve a notice should not be reflexive. The right approach is to read the wider position first, and then decide whether serving is the right move, and if so, which notice and with what proposed terms.

The questions that drive that read include:

  • Where is the market relative to the passing rent? If the market rent is now materially above the lease rent, the landlord has the reason to act. If it is materially below, the tenant does. If the two are broadly in line, there may be no urgency on either side.
  • What is the strategic objective for the property? A landlord planning to redevelop, refurbish, or take possession needs to use the statutory grounds correctly and on the right timetable. A tenant planning to stay long-term wants different terms to a tenant planning to exit at the next opportunity.
  • What does the current lease actually say? Some leases have provisions that affect the timing or form of notice. Others have already been varied or supplemented in ways that change the position. The exercise starts with the lease itself, not with the general assumption of how things work.
  • What evidence is available? Serving a notice with proposed terms is more effective when the proposed terms can be defended on market evidence. Serving on an unsupported figure invites a stronger response and a weaker eventual position.

Each of these questions has a different answer for each party. The strategic value of acting first comes from being the one who has worked through the answers and is ready to act on them.

Section 06
The cost of not deciding

The most common outcome in commercial lease renewals is that neither party serves. The contractual term expires, the lease continues on the existing terms under the protection of the 1954 Act, and the tenant continues paying the existing rent. Where the market rent is above the existing rent, that benefits the tenant. Where it is below, it benefits the landlord. Either way, one party is leaving value on the table, and they often do not know they are doing it.

Serving first is not always the right answer. Sometimes the strategic position is best served by leaving the lease to drift, particularly where the existing terms are favourable. But that should be an active decision based on the evidence, not a default position caused by not engaging with the question.

The decision deserves the same analytical attention as any other commercial decision affecting the asset or the occupational position. Treating it as administrative is usually the most expensive way to handle it.
