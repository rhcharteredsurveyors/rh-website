Rent Review

A rent review is not the landlord naming a price.

The figure you receive is an opening position, not an instruction. Here is how the process actually works, and where the room to negotiate sits.

13th May 2026 7 min read
Section 01
The common misunderstanding

One of the most consistent misunderstandings in commercial property is what a rent review actually is. The letter arrives proposing a new figure, and the assumption from the tenant side is often that the choice is binary: accept it, or walk away.

That is not how the process works. A rent review is a contractual mechanism for re-setting the rent under the terms of the existing lease. What rent should apply is a question of evidence, lease interpretation, and valuation. The landlord's proposed figure is one party's opening position. It is not, on its own, the answer.

The difference between treating the proposed figure as a decision to accept or refuse, and treating it as a starting position to be tested, is usually the difference between paying market rent and paying somewhere above it for the next several years.

Section 02
What the rent should reflect

The lease sets the rules.

Every rent review is governed by the lease that contains it. The clause defines the basis on which the new rent is to be assessed, and almost always anchors that basis to an open market rent at the review date.

What that means in practice is a hypothetical exercise. The valuation asks: if the property were available to let on the review date, on the terms of the existing lease, with a willing landlord and a willing tenant negotiating at arm's length, what rent would be agreed?

That is the figure the review is trying to find. It is not what the landlord wants. It is not what the tenant can afford. It is the rent that the market evidence supports, adjusted for the specific lease and the specific property.

Most leases also contain a series of assumptions and disregards that shape the exercise. Common assumptions include vacant possession, that the tenant has complied with their lease covenants, and that the property is available to let on the assumed terms. Common disregards include the goodwill of the tenant's business and any improvements the tenant has carried out at their own cost. These assumptions and disregards are not throwaway clauses. They materially affect what the resulting rent looks like, and a proper negotiation engages with them carefully.

Section 03
Where the evidence comes from

Market rent is evidenced, not asserted.

The strength of any rent review position rests on the evidence behind it. Opinion without evidence does not hold up in negotiation, and it certainly does not hold up at arbitration or independent expert determination if the matter ends up there.

Useful evidence usually falls into one of three categories.

  • Comparable lettings. Recent open market lettings of similar properties in the same locality, on broadly comparable lease terms. These carry the most weight when they are close to the review date, transactionally clean, and unaffected by special factors.
  • Comparable rent reviews and renewals. Settled reviews and lease renewals on comparable properties provide secondary evidence. They tend to carry less weight than open market lettings because settlements reflect negotiation as well as market value, but they are still valuable.
  • Other supporting information. Asking rents on currently marketed properties, take-up data, vacancy rates, and broader market commentary can all add context, although none of these are direct evidence of market rent on their own.

A good rent review position starts with the comparable evidence, works through the adjustments needed to reflect the differences between the comparable properties and the subject property, and ends at a figure that can be defended on its own terms.

The strongest rent review positions are the ones grounded in evidence from the outset. Not to inflate or suppress the figure, but to understand where it is likely to land.

Both sides of a rent review have access to the same market. The position that wins is the one that reads the evidence most accurately and presents it most clearly, not the one that holds out longest.

Section 04
The adjustments that matter

Comparables are rarely directly comparable.

The point that most discussions get wrong is that comparable evidence almost never comes from a property that is identical to the one being valued. The work is in identifying the differences and quantifying them.

Differences that typically need to be reflected include the length of the lease term, the presence and timing of break clauses, repairing and insuring obligations, any rent free periods or fitting-out incentives granted in the original deal, the basis of measurement applied, and the location and configuration of the property itself.

A comparable letting that on the face of it appears to suggest a higher rent might, once those adjustments are made, support a position significantly closer to the existing rent. The reverse is equally true. The headline figure on a comparable is a starting point. The adjustments are where the actual valuation work happens.

This is also why proposed figures from either side can be supported by superficially similar evidence and still be far apart. The figures themselves are not the disagreement. The adjustments to the underlying evidence are.

Section 05
What a good process looks like

Handled properly, a rent review should move from polarised opening positions to an evidenced common ground. The work that gets a review there is straightforward in principle: identify the lease terms that govern the exercise, gather the best available comparable evidence, make the necessary adjustments, and present a position that an independent third party would accept if the matter went that far.

The figure the review settles at should be defensible against the evidence, not the loudest assertion or the most patient resistance. Where reviews drift or end up at figures that cannot be supported, it is usually because one or both sides have skipped the evidence work and treated the negotiation as a question of position rather than analysis.

The proposed figure that arrives on the letter is the start of that process. It is rarely the end of it.
