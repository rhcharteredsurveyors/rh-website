Business Rates

Sometimes the right advice is not to challenge.

Every rates assessment deserves critical analysis before a challenge is launched. Done properly, that analysis sometimes lands on a recommendation to leave things alone, and there are good reasons for that.

27th May 2026 7 min read
Section 01
The default pitch

The standard offer from a lot of business rates firms is built on a single, confident idea: you're probably paying too much, and a challenge will get the figure down. Engagement is usually no-win-no-fee, charged as a percentage of the saving.

That model creates a particular incentive. Cases need to go ahead for the firm to get paid, so the analysis tends to point one way. Risks that might argue against a challenge get less airtime than they should.

The honest position is that rates work should start with a proper read of the assessment and the evidence around it, with no foregone conclusion either way. Sometimes that read leads to a challenge that materially reduces the liability. Sometimes it leads to advice not to proceed, because the risk of opening the file outweighs the available upside.

Knowing which case you're in is the actual job. Treating every assessment as a candidate for challenge misses the point.

Section 02
Why it isn't a one-way door

The Valuation Office can revise upwards.

The Check, Challenge, Appeal process exists to test whether a rateable value is correct. It's not a unidirectional route to a reduction. Once an assessment is in play, the Valuation Office can examine everything that sits behind the figure: the floor areas they hold, the analysis they've applied, the comparable evidence they've relied on, and any features of the property that should affect the valuation.

The point that gets glossed over in the standard pitch is that this review can correct things in either direction. If the existing assessment has been generous to the occupier (under-counted floor area, applied a soft rate per square foot, missed elements that should have been valued), a challenge brings those errors into view. The figure can go up.

The biggest risk in rates work is not failing to challenge. It's challenging something you shouldn't have.

That risk is asymmetric. A successful challenge might shave a few thousand off the annual liability. An unsuccessful one that triggers an upward revision can add the same amount, every year, for the rest of the rating list. The downside lasts longer than the upside, and it compounds.

Whether that risk is real in any specific case depends on what the existing record actually says, and how it compares to the underlying reality of the property. Which is where the analysis matters.

Section 03
How an assessment is built

What the figure actually represents.

The rateable value of a commercial property is, in essence, an estimate of its annual open-market rent at a fixed valuation date. For the 2026 rating list, which came into force on 1st April 2026, that date is 1st April 2024. Everything in the assessment is anchored to that point: the floor area, the rate per square foot applied, the adjustments for the specific property, and the evidence base of comparable lettings drawn from around 1st April 2024.

The figure is built from three main components, and a challenge can attack any of them, provided the evidence supports the attack:

  • The physical measurement. Floor area, ancillary space, and the basis on which the measurement was taken. The recorded figure needs to reflect not just the gross footprint of the property but the area that actually counts under the measurement standard applied. Subsequent works, internal alterations, or changes in use can leave the recorded area out of step with what the property now contains. If the recorded figure is higher than it should be, there is a route to reduction. If it is lower, opening the file invites correction in the other direction.
  • The rate per square foot. Drawn from comparable lettings and other assessments in the locality, adjusted for the specifics. If your applied rate is materially above the market evidence, there's a case to make. If it's already below market, the case runs the other way.
  • Property-specific adjustments. End-of-terrace location, return frontage, internal layout, condition, services, and similar factors. Many of these can be argued in either direction depending on what's already reflected in the assessment.

A genuine challenge needs to identify a specific element of one or more of these components that is demonstrably wrong, and have the evidence to support correction. The strength of that evidence is what determines whether a challenge will succeed, and what the downside risk looks like if it doesn't.

Section 04
When to challenge, when not to

The questions that actually matter.

Three questions sit at the heart of any rates review. The answers to all three drive the recommendation.

1. Is the assessment above defensible market evidence?

Not slightly above. Materially above, with comparable lettings, other assessments, or other documentary evidence that an independent valuer would accept. Soft challenges built on optimism rather than evidence tend to fail, and they expose the file to the Valuation Office's own review at the same time.

2. Where do the errors in the existing record sit?

This is where the real analysis happens, and it's the part that the standard pitch tends to skip. Errors in the existing record can run in either direction. If the Valuation Office is holding a floor area that's too high, or has applied a layout assumption that's been corrected by subsequent alterations, those are routes to reduction. Errors that work against the occupier are a reason to challenge. Errors that work in the occupier's favour are a reason to be cautious, because they're exactly the things a reassessment will surface.

3. What is the downside risk if the file is opened?

Once a check is submitted and the Valuation Office begins their own review, the scope of that review isn't limited to the points you've raised. They can look at everything. The honest question is whether you would be comfortable with the result if they did. If the answer is yes, the path is clear. If the answer involves any meaningful uncertainty, the calculation changes.

Section 05
What good advice looks like

The argument here isn't against challenging rates. Real overpayments exist. The Valuation Office gets things wrong. Comparable evidence shifts as the market moves. Properties that haven't been looked at in years can carry assessments that no longer reflect reality. There's genuine value in the work when the case is there.

The argument is for doing the analysis before the decision rather than after. An independent read of the assessment, the evidence, and the risk on both sides, given honestly, is what allows a sensible call to be made. That call is sometimes to challenge. Sometimes it's to leave the matter alone. Sometimes it's to address the wider commercial position (relocation, restructuring, planning for the next rating list) rather than the rateable value itself.

The right outcome from a rates review is the one that protects the position, not the one that generates a fee.

That's the difference between rates work pitched as a transaction and rates work pitched as advice. The first will almost always recommend a challenge. The second won't, because sometimes the analysis says no.
