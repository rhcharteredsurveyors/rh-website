Home Insights Repair obligations under a commercial lease
Lease Advisory

The repairing clause is one of the most fundamental aspects of a commercial lease.

Both parties need to understand what they are agreeing to, who is responsible for what, and how those responsibilities should be managed during the term. Too often they are not.

15th April 2026 8 min read
Section 01
Understanding what you are agreeing to

Repairing obligations sit at the centre of every commercial lease, and they are routinely under-engaged with on both sides. Tenants are usually experts at running their own business. They are far less often experts at running a commercial property, and the responsibilities that come with the lease can drift accordingly. Landlords often assume the tenant knows what they are doing, which is the wrong assumption to start from.

The result is that required works, whether ongoing maintenance, statutory compliance, or addressing the early signs of disrepair, can sit unaddressed for years. When the issue finally surfaces, it is usually larger and more expensive to resolve than it would have been if it had been picked up early. Neither side usually wants that outcome, but the structure of the lease makes it easy to drift into.

There is a related point about workmanship. Where a tenant is responsible for repairs, they will often instruct their own contractors, or rely on existing contacts, to carry out the work. Good outcomes depend on appropriate trades being used for the right job. Where they are not, an attempted repair can leave the property in a worse condition than it started, and create longer-term liability that neither party anticipated.

On the tenant side, the repairing covenant is often treated as a synonym for "we look after the property", with the assumption that this means routine maintenance and handing it back broadly as it was at the start. The obligation is usually wider than that. On the landlord side, the same covenant is often treated as a sealed promise that the tenant will absorb every cost the property generates, which is closer to the legal position but ignores how those obligations actually get managed in practice.

The first step on either side is making sure both parties understand the extent of the obligation and where it sits. That starts with the definition of the demise. A lease will typically define the property by reference to a plan and a written description. If the plan simply edges the boundary red and the description follows the edged area, the demise can include everything within that line, including external areas, even where the building only occupies part of it.

Whether the lease is full repairing and insuring (FRI) or internal repairing and insuring (IRI) then determines the scope of the tenant's repair obligation against that demise. Under FRI, the tenant is responsible for the structure as well as the interior. Under IRI, the tenant is typically only responsible for the internal parts of the demise, with the landlord retaining responsibility for the structure and exterior, although this is usually recovered through a service charge or other mechanism. The terms FRI and IRI rarely appear as shorthand on the face of the lease. The position is set by the repairing clause itself, read against the definition of the demise and any related schedules.

Both parties should be clear at the outset on the extent of the demise, the basis of the repairing obligation, and which party is responsible for which elements. Where that conversation does not happen at signing, it tends to happen years later, in less constructive circumstances.

Section 02
What FRI actually requires

"Put" and "keep" in repair.

A standard repairing covenant typically requires the tenant to keep the property in good and substantial repair, to make good any wants of repair, and to yield it up at the end of the term in the condition required by the lease. The wording looks simple. The legal interpretation behind it is significant, and the cumulative effect of that interpretation is wider than most tenants assume.

Four points are worth understanding.

  • The difference between "keep" and "put" in repair. On the face of it, the wording looks similar. The practical effect is not. An obligation to "keep" the property in repair, which is the more common wording, requires the tenant to maintain the property in no worse a state than the condition in which it was taken. That sounds modest, but where something has broken or worn out, keeping it in repair can require replacing it entirely with new, which can be a significant burden. An obligation to "put" the property into repair goes further. It requires the tenant to rectify any disrepair, including disrepair that existed before the lease started. A tenant taking on a building in poor condition under a "put" obligation must bring it up to good repair regardless of the starting state. This wording is less common, and is sometimes agreed as part of an incentive deal on the wider lease terms, but a tenant should read the repairing clause very carefully to understand which version applies.
  • "Good and substantial repair" is an objective standard. It is not "the condition the tenant thinks is fine". It is what an independent surveyor, applying the legal test, would consider appropriate for a property of that age, character, and locality. That standard can be considerably higher than informal expectations.
  • Repair includes replacement where repair is impractical. If a roof has reached the end of its serviceable life and patching it is no longer reasonable, the obligation can extend to replacing it. The lease does not cap the spend.
  • The starting condition can be limited by a schedule of condition. Unless the property is genuinely new, a tenant taking a lease of an older or imperfect property would usually be advised to enter into a schedule of condition, which limits the repair obligation to no worse than the condition recorded at the start. This is dealt with properly in the next section, because it is the single most effective tool a tenant has on this issue.

None of this is to say every tenant will end up paying for a roof replacement, or facing the maximum interpretation of every clause. It is to say that the obligation, as drafted, allows for outcomes that many tenants do not anticipate when they sign, and that the protections that exist need to be put in place at the start.

Section 03
The schedule of condition

The most important limiting document.

The single most effective tool a tenant has to manage repairing risk is a schedule of condition, prepared and annexed to the lease at the start. It records the condition of the property at the date the lease is granted, usually with photographs and a written description, and limits the tenant's repairing obligation to no worse than the condition recorded.

A schedule of condition does not absolve the tenant of the obligation to maintain the property during the term. It limits the standard the tenant has to deliver at the end of the term to the standard recorded at the start. A property that comes in with worn carpets, dated decoration, and visible wear on the fabric does not have to be handed back better than that.

The cost of preparing a schedule of condition is almost always small relative to what it saves. And it is almost always cheaper to argue from a document than from memory.

Schedules of condition are most commonly used in older properties, secondary stock, and buildings where the tenant has obvious concerns about the starting state. They are less common in new builds, but the principle of agreeing what condition the property is in at the start has value in any context.

The trade-off, from a landlord perspective, is that a schedule of condition caps the rebuilding of value at the end of the lease. The benefit, for both sides, is that it makes the eventual dilapidations exercise much faster and cheaper to resolve, because there is a documented baseline to argue from rather than a contested narrative about what the property looked like several years earlier.

Section 04
The end of the lease

Dilapidations is where the theory meets the bill.

Most repairing obligations are tested at the end of a lease, when the landlord serves a schedule of dilapidations setting out what they say the tenant has failed to do. The figures in those schedules can be substantial. They are also frequently disputed, frequently negotiated down, and frequently settled at amounts materially below the original claim.

That is not because landlords inflate their schedules deliberately. It is because the legal framework for dilapidations is more constrained than the lease wording on its own suggests. Section 18(1) of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1927 caps the recoverable damages at the diminution in value of the landlord's reversion caused by the tenant's breaches. If the landlord is going to refurbish or redevelop the property anyway, the tenant's breaches may have caused no real loss, and the recoverable figure can be much lower than the schedule suggests.

From the tenant side, that means a dilapidations claim is rarely a final number. It is a starting position that can usually be tested. From the landlord side, it means the realistic recoverable figure may bear little resemblance to the cost of putting the property back to lease standard. Both sides benefit from approaching the dilapidations exercise as a valuation question, not a building cost question.

The further point is that dilapidations work is more effective when it starts well before the lease ends. A tenant approaching the last twelve months of a lease has options, including carrying out works themselves, negotiating a financial settlement, or surrendering early on agreed terms. A tenant who waits until the lease has expired has fewer of those options open to them.

Section 05
Reading the clause properly

The practical point underneath all of this is that the repairing clause sits at the centre of the long-term economics of a commercial lease, and most negotiations treat it as boilerplate. The rent gets argued. The break clause gets argued. The repairing position is often left as drafted and the implications are absorbed later, at a point when neither side has much room to renegotiate.

A good lease negotiation reads the repairing clause carefully against the actual property and the actual occupational plan. For tenants, that usually means asking whether a schedule of condition is appropriate, whether the standard of repair is realistic given the existing fabric, and whether there are specific items, plant, services, or external elements, that need carving out. For landlords, it means understanding which protections are needed and which are illusory, and where the negotiating value sits.

The clause itself is rarely complex. The implications of it are. Both sides are better off engaging with the implications at the point of signing than discovering them when the lease ends.
